How the Game Works

A calm, visual introduction to each idea.
Rule
The Board
Eight by eight.
A chessboard has 64 squares — 8 ranks (rows) numbered 1–8, and 8 files (columns) labelled a–h. Every square has a unique name. White always plays from ranks 1 and 2. The board must be placed with a light square in the bottom-right corner.

"In chess, the board is the world, the pieces are the phenomena of the universe."— Thomas Huxley

Rule
Game Setup
Same start. White always moves first.
Rooks in the corners. Knights beside them. Bishops next. Queen on her own colour — white queen on white, black queen on black. King fills the last square. Pawns fill the entire second rank. White always makes the first move.

"Chess is the art which expresses the science of logic."— Mikhail Botvinnik, World Chess Champion

Rule
Capturing
Land on their square. They're gone.
To capture an enemy piece, move one of your pieces onto the square it occupies. The captured piece is removed from the board permanently. You cannot capture your own pieces.

"We cannot resist the fascination of sacrifice, since a passion for sacrifices is part of a chess player's nature."— Rudolf Spielmann

Rule
Check
Your King is under attack.
When your King is threatened by an enemy piece, you are in check. You must deal with it immediately — move the King away, block the attack, or capture the attacker. You cannot ignore check or make any other move.

"Chess makes man wiser and clear-sighted."— Barack Obama

Rule
Checkmate
No escape. Game over.
Checkmate occurs when the King is in check with no legal escape. He cannot move away, the attack cannot be blocked, and the attacker cannot be captured. The game ends immediately.

"The King is a fighting piece. Use it."— Wilhelm Steinitz, First World Chess Champion

Rule
Stalemate
A draw — not a win.
If it is your turn to move but you have no legal moves and your King is not in check, the game is a draw. This is stalemate. A winning player can accidentally stalemate their opponent and throw away the win.

"Chess, like love, like music, has the power to make us happy."— Siegbert Tarrasch

Rule
Illegal Moves
You cannot walk into danger.
You can never make a move that leaves your own King in check. Here the King cannot go to d1 or d2 (controlled by the Rook) or f1 (controlled by the Bishop). Any move exposing your King is simply not allowed.

"Chess is not about the pieces on the board. It's about what happens between them."— Anonymous
